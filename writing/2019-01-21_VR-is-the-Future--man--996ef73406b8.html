VR is the Future, man.

I am not a thought leader.

I’m not even really that thoughtful.

But holy shit do I love VR.

I was invited today to go to a VR Arcade in Green Bay, about an hour north of where I live. I can safely say I was happy I went. The staff was crazy welcoming and all-in-all just a really great group of nerds. I mean, of course they’re nerds. They’re running VR Arcade. So obviously we automatically felt right at home.

This, however, wasn’t my first VR experience. I had previously been to another VR experience. I wouldn’t call it so much an arcade, because you only got to pick between two games. And it certainly wasn’t a recent installation. It was at one of the water parks in Wisconsin Dells, I can’t remember exactly which right now.

Now, don’t get me wrong, it was certainly an eye opening experience. Maybe not worth the 60 bucks I paid to do it, but definitely worth it just to catch a glimpse of the magic.

What magic, you ask? There is something intangible, something so incredibly interesting about VR to me. I think it crops up in these little movements and moments that don’t feel like anything else I’ve ever done in a game before.

This particular game, at the VR Experience in the Dells, was a zombie shooter. First, my mask was kind of clouding up and it was funneling most of my breathing air straight up at the screen. Poor headset design, bad-ish sound. And even with all these things that destroyed the immersion of the game, I still found myself jumping when a zombie broke through a door, or rushed at me.

That’s that magic I was telling you about. It has this ability to produce really visceral, gut moving feelings. Vertigo, panic. All of these crazy feelings that are closer to real, intense emotions. I remember there was a tiny elevator that brought you up to the second story of the game.

As I moved up, I fully expected the floor beneath my feet to be moving and shaking.

I left that experience with a newfound appreciation for VR. It convinced me then, even. However I hadn’t pursued the thought anymore. I can’t possibly afford to spend 60 dollars every time I wanted to experience it. And at the time a lot of the home VR setups seemed lackluster, and development hadn’t caught up with the hardware quite yet, I think.

But today? Today was a whole different story.

The headset fit snugly, and I was even provided with a little spacer that let me keep my glasses on during it. (That was a no-go in the last one) The space was new and clean and ultimately quite well done, even with an area for easy restaurant type food. Pizza, fries, that sort of thing.

And the games. Man, the games.

The first we played was Quiver, basically human tower defense. Large mobs of monsters come running at you and you have only your trusty magic bow and arrow.

It was so incredibly entertaining to try to figure out the mechanics of a game like this. In a computer game your interaction with the mechanics is so incredibly limited. Press and hold, release to fire.

In Quiver you actually had to reach down into your quiver, grab an arrow, and nock it to your bow, then draw it, and fire it. Hoping to hit one of the massive horde of animals attacking you and your friends.

Oh yeah, I forgot to mention, your friends are in there with you, too. Teleporting from perch to perch, calling out when monsters are getting too close to your gates. It was incredibly fun.

Then came beatsaber. The quintessential music-DDR-esque timing game where you attempt to swipe through 3D blocks flying at you with your two lightsabers.

It’s incredibly fun. It’s just fun. It’s hard for me to remember the last time I had this much fun with games. Sure games are ‘fun’, they’re definitely entertaining. But this was just so different, getting your whole body into it has something to do with it. I found myself dancing a little bit along to the beat of the game. It was incredible.

And last, but definitely not least, was my favorite. Super Hot VR. Now I had heard of and seen tons of videos for Super Hot for the PC. The slow down mechanics looked awesome, and I loved the art styling of the game, so I had to see how it looked in VR.

It’s amazing.

The game menu is simply you in a room, filled with steampunk-esque computers. You load a floppy disk into the drive, and a white light shines down on you. Looking up — physically tilting your head back and looking above you — you realize there’s another VR helmet, hanging in the room you’re in. Reaching up, you pull it down over your head, and the game begins.

How fucking cool is that?!

The game was both challenging and incredibly entertaining. The permadeath features of SuperHot lend it to attempting the same task over and over and over again, and it never got tiring.

The only unfortunate thing was as always: all good things must come to an end. Our little timer went off, and our games were shut down.

And I was left just being blown away.

I’m already looking at graphics cards and processor upgrades for my PC.

I see more VR in the future. At least for me.
