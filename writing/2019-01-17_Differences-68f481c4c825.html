Differences

A topic I’ve been thinking about lately is differences, or contrast. Not in any particular sense. I think this concept of difference is integral to how we view the world.

For example, let’s say you’re having a bank issue, or perhaps a package of yours has been lost. You decide to take the most dreaded course of action: you’re going to call them. On the phone.

I can feel the pit of fear in my stomach already twisting and turning. I don’t call people on the phone. Only in the most dire of circumstances do I resort to this horrible experience. It takes too long, it’s often extremely unproductive, generally useless. I hate phone calls.

But imagine for a moment that this phone call is a bit different. You dial the number and instead of being greeted with that typical monontonous robot, telling you to dial 1 or 4 or 7 or whatever random combination of numbers they’ve decided on, you get a girl.

A real life girl. Her name is Melissa and she sounds entirely too happy to be working in customer support, but you’re kind of thankful for that.

She says “Hello! What can I do for you today?”

And you say “Well, uh — “, obviously at this point you weren’t even prepared to interact with a human being. It’s unfathomable that something like this could happen, and so you haven’t even begun to state your case. “Well I think I’ve lost a package.”

And before you can even start again Melissa says “Oh no! I’ve already got your account pulled up, is it your latest order?”

You say “Yes.”

And she says “Don’t worry about it, we’ll send another one out for you this afternoon with rush shipping. We’re terribly sorry, something must have happened in transit. Anything else I can help you with today?”

“Uh, err- No, I guess. I don’t think so?”

Melissa has just customer-service-jiu-jitsued your face. And you loved every moment of it, because it was utterly and incredibly different than your expectations.

In fact it was better. In every possible way. And this provides you with this amazing experience, and I think great companies are built on this principle.

My first boss at the hotel I used to work at had this rule called the “Plus Plus” rule. I don’t know where he got it, maybe it’s some contrived notion from the back of some shit managerial book.

But he said every time you interact with a guest, you wanted to leave them thinking that they got what they wanted plus a little bit more.

If the received experience is beyond the expectation, the difference between the two has a simple name: Value.

It’s this difference in expectation that builds the whole picture of the experience in a person’s mind. If the expectation is exactly met, then the experience is completely unremarkable. It went according to plan, see?

But if the expectation is exceeded, or god forbid, not met? The experience becomes something different.

So for example, if you can take an excessive painful process and make it bearable, that’s a difference. Maybe that’s enough to get some attention, because the expectation is that it’s painful, and you’ve removed some of that pain.

What happens then if someone else, a competitor, could come along and not only make the experience painless — what if they made it enjoyable?

Well that’s something different entirely again. And they’ve just moved the bar.
