What’s The Story Morning Glory?

Names are important. Starting with the name seems like the most logical place to begin. The original potato was in fact of the sweet variety (more on this later), and called patata by the Spanish. This is a hybridization of the Taíno and Quechuan words batata and papa, respectively.

At one point Taíno was the primary language spoken in the Caribbean, while Quechuan is from the Andes: The Birthplace of the Potato. While Taíno is largely extinct at this point, Quechuan is a thriving language, with some seven million speakers remaining in Peru.

Peru in fact houses the earliest known archaeological remains of potato tubers, at Ancón, dating to 2500BC. It’s in South America that potatoes were first domesticated and cultivated.

Potatoes were one of the primary foodstuffs of the Incan Empire, who powered their armies via a dish called chuño or papas secas, a specialized process of freeze-drying potatoes. Potatoes selected for chuño are laid out in an even layer on the ground, and subjected to the cold temperatures in the mountains, and allowed to dry during the daytime sun. The potatoes are then transported to another flat area, and stomped or danced on by whole families, thereby squeezing the rest of the water from the potato’s flesh. What results is a freeze-dried — and shelf-stable — potato that can potentially last decades.

But I digress, chuño isn’t even the most interesting part about potatoes! Spanish sailors, after exploring and mining in South America presumably returned to Spain with potatoes and maize in the bellies of their ships, thus introducing potatoes to Europe and placing the potato in a position to become today’s fourth largest food crop.

But it wasn’t that easy. Potatoes were largely spread for reasons not entirely related to food. It was suspected that the tuber held medicinal properties. Shakespeare thought it was an aphrodesiac. These beliefs caused Philip II of Spain to send a few potatoes to the Pope. Yep. A gift of potatoes. The Pope then sent the tubers to his papal ambassador in the Netherlands, who then planted them willy nilly around Europe.

Spain was also one of the first countries in Europe to also embrace potatoes as a way of fueling their armies protecting their empire. Peasants, being introduced to potatoes via the traveling Spanish armies, were untrusting of potatoes, which were then typically grown with other herbs and medicines as opposed to in the fields where traditional grains were cultivated. They nicknamed potatoes “The Devil’s Apples”.

Skipping some of the less interesting bits, here’s the highlights:

  • Marie Antoinette once wore a headdress of potato flowers to a ball.
  • Fredrick The Great attempted to quell the peasant’s distaste for potatoes, proclaiming in his ‘Potato Order’ that the tubers were “a very nutritious food supplement.”. He is affectionately known as the Kartoffelkönig (“potato king”).
  • The Irish Potato Famine (not a conspiracy) caused around one million Irish to emigrate. Ireland’s population declined by nearly 25% due to death and this emigration. That’s nuts.

Taxonomy And You

Solanum Tuberosum

There are about 5,000 varieties of potato world wide. This 5,000 does not include the sweet potato. That’s right. Our orange friend isn’t actually part of the same family as our white-fleshed savory potato pals. Without getting into some sciency-stuff — though don’t get me wrong I love me some sciency stuff — there was just a cool little connection between the two family names. Despite being both called potatoes, the white-fleshed potato that most of us associate with ‘potato’ is called Solanum Tuberosum and is part of the Nightshade family. The Nightshades include various agricultural and medicinal plants, including the tomato, and the Capsicum family! Peppers! Why is this important? I use important very loosely here.

Well, the sweet potato belongs to the Convolvulaceae family. Otherwise known as the Morning Glory family. I find it endlessly entertaining that the sweet ‘potato’ and potato are also Morning Glory and Nightshade.

The Silent Killer

They’re lurking in the dark corners of your kitchen. You know the ones I’m talking about. The places where you put them after accidentally buying too many for that one recipe you found. You actually bring them into your own home. That’s right. They’re sitting there, biding their time. Growing more deadly by the minute. Waiting for their moment to strike.

Yes, it’s potatoes.

No, for real that’s actually what’s happening.

I’ll be honest. I’ve almost commited suicide by potato. You probably have, too. Let me explain:

Alongside Solanum Tuberosum in the Nightshade family is none other than belladonna, henbane, and tobacco. The foliage and berries of these plants tend to be extremely toxic to the human body. And so are potatoes. Sometimes. Specifically the green plant parts and flowers of potatoes are in fact toxic to humans.

Potatoes (among other members of the Solanaceae) contain glycoalkaloids, like solanine. Solanine acts as a natural pesticide, deterring prey that would like to munch on some potato leaves. In humans symptoms of solanine poisoning include:

  • Vomiting
  • Fever or lower than normal body temperature (hypothermia)
  • Headache
  • Slow pulse and/or breathing
  • Stomach Pain
  • Diarrhea
  • Hallucinations
  • Delirium
  • Shock
  • Vision Changes

Now, before you never eat another potato, let me tell you how this works. I’m certain you’ve left a potato alone for a little too long. It starts to grow and change and mutate. Looks like something from the Alien series, right? Eventually it starts to grow sprouts. Now, the most important part, is the coloration. Basically all parts of the potato plant that are green have a higher concentration of solanine, and thus are unsafe for humans to eat. It’s the potato’s hulk mode.

TL DR: Potatoes are cool, don’t eat green ones.
