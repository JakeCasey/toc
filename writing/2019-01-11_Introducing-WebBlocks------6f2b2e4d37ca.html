Introducing WebBlocks. . .

(A potential future project for Jake Casey)

Analog creation for a digital world.

As a software developer our work is incredibly intangible. Almost nothing we build has any component beyond the visual. There’s no touch, no texture, no sense of weight or of feeling.

And yet we build incredible, beautiful things. We’ve mastered this art of seeing something in the ether, and chipping away at a marble block until we’ve reached our goal. Web development and design is an incredibly creative process, and according to Austin Kleon of “Steal like an Artist”, analog components are important in the creative process.

He even goes so far as to have two totally separate workspaces, one in which there are no digital devices allowed, and the other where he has his computer and ability to publish and share his experience.

And so I thought “I’m a web developer, but I can’t really draw. I’m pretty bad at that. And I’d almost bet that a lot of other devs are bad ‘artists’ too.”

So how can I create a website in analog?

Introducing WebBlocks. The easiest way I could explain this idea is it’s building blocks for websites. Physical wooden blocks. It would come with a page, stylized to look like a browser. Maybe it would have woodburned lines designating the URL bar and icons at the top. It would be inset maybe a quarter of an inch.

And next to you would be sat this organized box of components. Inputs, navigation bars, links. Icons, pagination, progress bars, alerts. All designed to fit within each other, and made of wood. These pieces would have a tactile sensation. You could smell the wood. You can feel the weight of the pieces as they slot into each other. Maybe even magnets that would properly align them to a grid.

Using these components you could physically move pieces around. Change the design and the layout of a site in real time without ever touching a keyboard. If you find a design you like, you can just snap a picture with your phone to have it for later. Then change it again.

I would imagine developers would have one of these next to them on their desk, or maybe on a separate space solely designed for this, where you can be physically creative in real time. It would give you the freedom to really play with code. To feel that joy of creation and planning without anything being in your way.

You wouldn’t even have to plan out whole sites this way. You could experiment with different layouts idly while thinking about your latest bug. You can display your favorite layouts on your desk for others to see and discuss.

I think it’s a pretty cool idea, and if anyone is interested please do let me know. I may talk to a woodworker when I get home to see what something like that might run me.
