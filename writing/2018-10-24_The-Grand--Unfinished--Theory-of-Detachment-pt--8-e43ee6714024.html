The Grand (Unfinished) Theory of Detachment pt. 8

Good morning, everyone. I had an idea of what to write about last night, and was going to write it down, but didn’t. So here we are. Grasping at straws. The Grand Theory it is!

But we’ve only got six minutes left, so we need to make this fast.

There is magic in Volume.

It appears everywhere. It’s brought about by any action done repeatedly. It is processes that are unconsciously honed. It’s focus. I think Volume is a key to consistent success. Isn’t that just practice you say? ‘Practice’ doesn’t encompass the scale of the act. Volume is aiming at a massive number of repetitions. Usually commensurate with the complexity of the act. I can ‘practice’ the piano a few times (I don’t play the piano, but I wish I did!) but it’s a sufficiently complicated skill that I could never just ‘get it’ on my first try. And maybe part of Volume is managing expectations, as well. You can’t expect to just ‘get it’ immediately. But what you can do is sit down every day and do it. The Volume. The Work.

With Volume comes Iteration. It’s this constant feedback loop, small and quick to respond to outside forces. Iteration is what you’ve learned, applied to something, again and again. In Volume. King Lear did not come out, word for word, finished. It required endless editing, iteration and meticulous crafting. This process of changing something after it’s reached a certain point in ‘completeness’ is essential for it to finally achieve it’s goals.

But there’s other weird things that happen around Volume and Iteration. For example, with Volume you create a massive backlog of work. This backlog of work retains (and gains!) its value over time. Let’s say you’re me: a mediocre writer. I’ve published a few things here and there. The key is what happens when one of the things you write, or pieces of software you build, really takes off. Maybe a new piece gains a little bit of virality. Gets shared a few times. If you look at the numbers: all your other pieces get a few views, too. People go back through your catalog, and check it out. So even those pieces in which you’re learning, you’re building, and honing. They have value too.

With Iteration it’s interesting, this concept of changing something, in small amounts. For example, do we know what changes were made to some of the greatest works of our time? The small choices and changes made behind the scenes? Were any of the unchanged versions better? I doubt it. Once we’ve made our opinion about, in this case a piece of writing, I think it would be hard to go back and say “Shakespeare’s first version was better.” And so you always have to make the changes. That’s where that the magic comes in. It’s the final snowflake that sets off the avalanche. It’s the final pass on the whetstone that makes the blade as sharp as it needs to be.

You cannot, and should not, expect perfection. Perfection is the enemy. You should shun it and spurn it. You must iterate, and you must change. There’s nothing beautiful that’s perfect.

Whatever your thing is, writing, coding, singing, piano, building businesses, underwater basket weaving, you need to do it in Volume. And when you see something has promise, you need to Iterate on it. Rinse and repeat.

Short and sweet today. I have to go to work, otherwise I’d hang out.

Love you, bye.
