What’s Alexa?

In case you’re woefully technologically illiterate, or reading this in the future when Alexa has died a slow and horrible death after she became self-aware and started a war with Google Home, let’s go over what Alexa actually is.

Alexa is an intelligent personal assistant developed by Amazon, first used in the Amazon Echo and the Amazon Echo Dot devices developed by Amazon Lab126. It is capable of voice interaction, music playback, making to-do lists, setting alarms, streaming podcasts, playing audiobooks, and providing weather, traffic, and other real-time information, such as news.[2] Alexa can also control several smart devices using itself as a home automation system. Users can extend the Alexa capabilities by installing “skills” (additional functionality developed by third-party vendors, in other settings more commonly called apps). — Wikipedia (yeah I just quoted Wikipedia, in your face English teachers.)

Custom Skills

Our first skill on the list happens to be the skill with the highest ‘skill’ cap. (haha). These are completely custom skills, designed by developers from the ground up.

A custom skill can handle any kind of request, so long as you can create the code to fulfill the request and provide appropriate data in the interaction model to let users invoke the request. This is the most flexible kind of skill you can build, but also the most complex, since you need to provide the interaction model.

This means that you build the intents that your skill can accept. An intent in this case is something like “Order a pizza” or “Call me an Uber”. After which, you would also build what happens on the back end, or what actually happens because of the intent. You could build something that says “Alexa, pick my Storytime articles for today.” and then she sends you your custom built podcast from our wide selection of RSS feeds. Smart Home Skills

Our second skill comes in with a framework that we can begin to build things on, however it’s probably one of the more niche skills, requiring actual hardware as well. This abstracts the controls of various home-based commands to a unified VPI (Voice program interface? Did I just make something cool up? Just kidding I checked they’re already using VUI which is close enough. Damn.) meaning you can just say “Alexa, turn on my microwave”. Because in the future our microwaves will be voice controlled too, imo. From the docs:

You (as the developer) define how your skill responds to a particular directive. For instance, you write the code that makes a light turn on when your skill receives a “turn on the light” directive. This code is hosted as an AWS Lambda function. Note that a skill built with the Smart Home Skill API can respond only to the requests (device directives) supported by the API.

If you’re a dev, there’s a little more you can dive into there, but if you’re a marketer, this skill probably won’t have much use for you. But it’s still freakin’ cool. Smart homes are gonna be the future, guarantee it.

Flash Briefing Skills

Here’s the skill that gets the most air time in Crushing It. Probably for good reason, as this is pretty in line with Gary V’s microcontent strategies. Making creative content that’s along the same lines as the rest of your social media presence, in small easily digestible experiences. Hopefully without making you mad, Gary, in the book you tell a small story that does an excellent job illustrating the potential of the flash briefing. It’s the story of an etiquette teacher, who, long story short, records themselves answering tons of common etiquette questions, and then forms intents for these in an Alexa Skill. So the user can just say “Hey Alexa, does the fork go on the left or right side of the plate?” And her voice would play with a little snippet of info instructing that it goes on the right side because of X reasons. I’m not an etiquette coach, this is an example, if you ruin your night with a head of state or a prime minister don’t yell at me, ok?

Video Skills

This one is pretty straightforward and seems less ‘interesting’ than the flash briefing, but it’s up to you to find new and novel ways to use it. Essentially it provides an intent that lets you play a video immediately. One of the best applications in my opinion would be cooking. “Alexa, show me how to julienne a carrot” and your skill plays just a clip explaining how to julienne, or debone a chicken, or how to make a roux. Simple, effective content that helps people. I always measure stuff by whether or not I’d use something. And I’d use that. If I had an Alexa. (If this goes viral Amazon you can send me one as thanks, I won’t mind.) I’ll probably end up picking one up soon anyways just to play around with this, so don’t worry if you can’t spare one.

List Skills

A list skill facilitates the use of list events in the skill service. Thus, the skill can understand and react to changes that happen to lists from top-level utterances on Alexa.
A list skill is created through the ASK CLI (Alexa Skills Kit Command Line Interface).
A list skill may include an optional custom component, which can include any features allowed for custom skills, in addition to list skill features.

I think the key bit is the last one. You can INCLUDE custom skills in your list skills. If you remember from a little bit ago, custom skills can do pretty much whatever you want them to, at the cost that you’ll probably need an actual developer to implement them. So now I’m thinking of something like “Alexa, add more cereal to my grocery list.” but this isn’t just any grocery list. This one’s practically Skynet, and it knows your favorite cereal is Cheerios, and it hooks up to some complex drone powered cereal delivery system, and it also knows that your milk is expired so it brings that, too. Or something equally cool. The point is, you can add something to a list and you can take actions on that list. That’s my understanding of how this’d work anyway.
